Density Field Dynamics (Optical Density Model)

Density Field Dynamics (DFD), also referred to as the Optical Density Model, proposes that gravitation, optical propagation, and quantum wavefunction collapse arise from a single scalar refractive field ψ(x,t) defined on flat Euclidean space. Matter accelerates via a = (c²/2)∇ψ while photons propagate with refractive index n = eψ, making the local light speed c₁ = c e−ψ. This unified optical framework reproduces all classical tests of General Relativity—light deflection, gravitational redshift, Shapiro delay, and perihelion advance—through refractive geometry alone, without invoking spacetime curvature. Earlier variable-light-speed ideas anticipated the concept, but never connected one-way light speed variation to energy density itself. Density Field Dynamics provides the first mathematically complete and experimentally testable formulation, establishing that optical density gradients govern both light propagation and gravitational acceleration.

Key Testable Prediction: Co-located optical cavities and atomic clocks transported across altitude exhibit a frequency-ratio slope ΔR/R = ξ(ΔΦ/c²) with ξ = +1 at the 10−15 level, in direct contradiction to General Relativity's prediction of ξ = 0. This constitutes a decisive, parameter-free experimental discriminator achievable with current optical clock technology.

Unexplained Bright–Dim Intensity Asymmetries in SOHO/UVCS Lyman-α Data

Note: Although published after the EM-coupling preprint, this analysis was conducted independently and without reference to DFD.
Permutation analysis of 334 daily SOHO/UVCS Lyman-α spectra (2007–2009) finds that 163 of 321 day–radial bins (51%) show statistically significant bright–dim intensity asymmetries at 5% FDR. The mean effect size is modest (Cohen’s d ≈ 0.24) but robust under resampling; corresponding Doppler-velocity contrasts are insignificant. The result was initially treated as unrelated to DFD EM coupling, though its characteristics are now recognized as potentially consistent with sectoral field-dependent modulation. Released as an open anomaly to encourage community investigation.
