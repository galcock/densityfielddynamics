\section{Pre-Registration Ethics Assessment}

\textbf{Finding 235}: Ethical considerations for the Euclid pre-registration.

The programme has identified three ethical concerns:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Self-blinding}: The analysis pipeline is written before Euclid DR1 access, but the predictions are based on N-body simulations that were \emph{not} blind. The predictions could, in principle, be adjusted to match prior expectations. \textbf{Mitigation}: The SHA-256 hash of the prediction file is published on arXiv \emph{before} Euclid DR1 release. Any post-hoc modification is detectable.

\item \textbf{Cherry-picking probes}: With 14 predictions, there is a look-elsewhere effect. If 13 are confirmed and 1 is not, is DFD confirmed or challenged? \textbf{Mitigation}: The pre-registration paper specifies the combined $\chi^2$ statistic as the primary metric, not individual predictions. The look-elsewhere effect is accounted for in the covariance matrix.

\item \textbf{Error bar inflation}: Setting conservative error bars increases the chance of ``confirmation'' but reduces the information content. \textbf{Mitigation}: Error bars are derived from the N-body suite (not inflated). The pre-registration paper publishes the derivation methodology.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Verdict}: The pre-registration protocol is ethically sound. The SHA-256 hash + published error bar methodology + combined $\chi^2$ criterion make post-hoc manipulation extremely difficult.
