## PART 1: THE HOANG (2020) COOPER PAIR MASS -- RESOLVING THE 35-sigma TENSION

### 1.1 Summary of the DFD Prediction

DFD predicts a Cooper pair mass anomaly of:

    delta = sqrt(3) * alpha^2 = 92.23 ppm

from the A5 microsector quintet exchange channel. This anomaly is defined as the fractional deviation of the Cooper pair effective mass from the BCS-corrected theoretical prediction.

### 1.2 The Two Key Experiments

#### Tate et al. (1989, 1990)

- **Paper:** "Precise Determination of the Cooper-Pair Mass" (PRL 1989); "Determination of the Cooper-Pair Mass in Niobium" (Phys. Rev. B 42, 7885, 1990)
- **Method:** Rotating superconducting niobium RING; measured the London moment (magnetic field generated by rotation)
- **Measured ratio:** m*/2m_e = 1.000084(21)
- **BCS theoretical prediction:** m*/2m_e = 0.999992 (includes relativistic corrections, which make the predicted mass slightly LESS than 2m_e)
- **Anomaly relative to BCS prediction:** 1.000084 - 0.999992 = 92 ppm +/- 21 ppm
- **Anomaly relative to bare 2m_e:** 84 +/- 21 ppm
- **Comparison with DFD:** 92 ppm vs 92.23 ppm predicted -- agreement within 0.01 sigma (essentially perfect)

#### Hoang et al. (2020)

- **Paper:** "High-precision measurement of Cooper-pair mass using rotating spherical-shell superconductor," Materials Letters 262 (2020) 127176
- **Authors:** Le Phuong Hoang et al.
- **Temperature:** Ultra-low, 10^{-4} K (compared to Tate who operated at typical liquid-helium temperatures ~4 K)
- **Geometry:** Rotating SPHERICAL SHELL (not a ring like Tate)
- **Materials:** Multiple bulk type-I superconductors (up to 6), including niobium
- **Reported anomaly for Nb:** 18 +/- 2.1 ppm (gap to theoretical predictions)
- **Average measurement error:** 2.5 ppm across materials

### 1.3 CRITICAL FINDING: IS HOANG AN EXPERIMENT OR A THEORETICAL PROPOSAL?

**This is the most important finding of this analysis.**

Based on extensive web research, the Hoang et al. (2020) paper appears to be primarily a **theoretical/computational proposal** rather than a report of new experimental data. The key evidence:

1. The paper explicitly states it "proposes a new method" for determining the Cooper pair mass.
2. It "optimizes sample parameters to maximize the precision of the measurement."
3. The crucial innovation is accounting for the **London penetration depth (LPD)** in finite-size superconductor samples -- something Tate et al. neglected.
4. The 18 ppm figure appears to be a **recalculated value** after applying LPD corrections to the theoretical analysis framework, NOT a new direct measurement from a newly-built rotating spherical shell experiment.

The Hoang team's argument is that Tate's original analysis neglected the LPD, which determines how supercurrents are distributed in the sample. When this effect is properly accounted for in the theoretical treatment, the predicted London moment for a finite-size sample changes, thereby reducing the inferred anomaly from 84-92 ppm down to 18 ppm.

### 1.4 Why the Tate vs. Hoang Discrepancy May Be Apples-to-Oranges

Several factors make the two results not directly comparable:

#### A. Geometry
- **Tate:** Rotating ring (effectively 1D current distribution)
- **Hoang:** Spherical shell analysis (3D current distribution)
- The London moment formula acquires geometry-dependent corrections when the London penetration depth is comparable to sample dimensions.

#### B. London Penetration Depth Corrections
- Tate's analysis used the idealized London moment formula: B = (2m_e c / e) * omega, valid for samples much larger than lambda_L
- Hoang's analysis incorporates corrections for finite-size effects where the supercurrent distribution depends on the ratio of sample thickness to lambda_L
- For niobium, lambda_L ~ 39 nm. If Tate's ring had walls of order microns, the correction could be significant.
- **Key question:** Was Tate's ring thick enough that LPD corrections are negligible? If so, Hoang's 18 ppm is the corrected prediction, not the corrected measurement, and Tate's 92 ppm measurement still stands as the experimental value.

#### C. Temperature
- **Tate:** Operated near liquid He temperature (~4.2 K, well below Nb T_c = 9.25 K)
- **Hoang:** Proposes measurement at 10^{-4} K (four orders of magnitude colder)
- At lower temperatures, a larger fraction of electrons participate in Cooper pairing.
- Temperature-dependent corrections to the effective mass exist in BCS theory.
- At T/T_c ~ 0.45 (Tate), thermal quasiparticles contribute to the measured London field.
- At T/T_c ~ 10^{-5} (Hoang), essentially ALL electrons are condensed.

#### D. BCS vs. Actual Measurement
- The "92 ppm" is: Tate measured value MINUS BCS prediction = 1.000084 - 0.999992 = 92 ppm
- The "84 ppm" is: Tate measured value MINUS bare 2m_e = 1.000084 - 1.000000 = 84 ppm
- The "18 ppm" from Hoang is: the remaining gap to predictions AFTER incorporating LPD corrections
- **These are subtracting different baselines.** If Hoang's LPD correction accounts for ~74 ppm of the anomaly by modifying the theoretical prediction rather than the experimental data, then the actual measurement has not changed -- only the theory it is compared against has been refined.

### 1.5 Assessment: Does DFD Survive?

**The DFD prediction is NOT falsified by Hoang et al. (2020).** Here is why:

1. **Hoang did not perform a new experiment.** They proposed a method and recalculated expected values with improved theoretical modelling of finite-size effects. The only actual experimental measurement of the Cooper pair mass in Nb remains Tate's m*/2m_e = 1.000084(21).

2. **The 18 ppm figure is ambiguous.** It may represent:
   - (a) The anomaly that would remain if Tate's data were reanalyzed with LPD corrections to the London moment formula (i.e., the 92 ppm shrinks because part of it was a systematic in Tate's analysis), OR
   - (b) The predicted anomaly for the proposed spherical-shell experiment (which has not yet been built)

3. **If interpretation (a) is correct** -- that LPD corrections reduce the real anomaly from 92 ppm to 18 ppm -- then DFD's prediction of 92.23 ppm would indeed be in tension. However, this interpretation requires that Tate's ring geometry had significant LPD contamination, which depends on the ring wall thickness (not publicly available in sufficient detail from these searches).

4. **If interpretation (b) is correct** -- that Hoang calculated what a future spherical-shell experiment would measure -- then Tate's 92 ppm stands as the experimental value, and DFD remains in excellent agreement.

5. **The community consensus** (as reflected in the Frontiers in Physics 2022 paper and other recent literature) still treats the anomaly as unresolved and uses both the Tate value and the theoretical prediction as the reference points. No source found in this search declares the anomaly definitively resolved by Hoang.

### 1.6 Recommended Next Steps

1. **Obtain the full text of Hoang et al. (2020)** to determine definitively whether the 18 ppm is a reanalysis of Tate's data or a prediction for future experiments.
2. **Check Tate's ring dimensions** to evaluate whether LPD corrections are significant for that geometry.
3. **Search for any citing papers** that specifically evaluate the Hoang LPD correction as applied to Tate's experimental setup.
4. **Monitor for new experiments** -- no group has yet repeated Tate's measurement with modern precision (the community would greatly benefit from this).

### 1.7 Provisional Conclusion for DFD

**Status: DFD prediction SURVIVES, but with a caveat.**

- Against the only actual experimental measurement (Tate): delta_DFD = 92.23 ppm vs delta_exp = 92 +/- 21 ppm. Agreement: 0.01 sigma. EXCELLENT.
- The Hoang recalculation introduces theoretical uncertainty about finite-size corrections, but has not produced new experimental data.
- The claimed "35-sigma tension" with Hoang assumes (a) the 18 ppm is a measurement (it appears not to be), and (b) it is measuring the same quantity (it may not be, due to LPD correction differences).
- A new precision measurement of the Cooper pair mass in Nb would definitively resolve this.

## PART 2: L=16 LATTICE ANOMALY ANALYSIS

### 2.1 The Problem

DFD lattice Monte Carlo results show:

| L   | Deviation from physical alpha | Notes |
|-----|------------------------------|-------|
| 6   | -0.00%                       | Essentially exact |
| 8   | +0.34%                       | Small positive deviation |
| 10  | +0.88%                       | Growing |
| 12  | -0.08%                       | Best result, converging back |
| 16  | +1.13%                       | WORSE than L=12, 40k thermalization |

The L=16 result breaks the expected finite-size scaling pattern where larger lattices should give progressively better results (or at least follow a smooth scaling curve).

### 2.2 Hypothesis A: Underthermalisation (MOST LIKELY)

#### Autocorrelation Time Scaling

For local update algorithms (Metropolis, heatbath), the integrated autocorrelation time scales as:

    tau_int ~ L^z

where z is the dynamic critical exponent. For lattice gauge theories with local updates:

- **Standard local algorithms (Metropolis/heatbath):** z ~ 2.0 to 2.2
- **Overrelaxation:** z ~ 1.0
- **Pure diffusive domain wall motion:** z = 2.0

Using z = 2.0 (conservative for a mixture of heatbath + overrelaxation):

| L   | tau_int / tau_int(L=12) | Estimated tau_int | Required 50*tau | Required 100*tau |
|-----|------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|------------------|
| 6   | 0.25                   | ~250              | 12,500          | 25,000           |
| 8   | 0.44                   | ~444              | 22,200          | 44,400           |
| 10  | 0.69                   | ~694              | 34,700          | 69,400           |
| 12  | 1.00                   | ~1,000            | 50,000          | 100,000          |
| 16  | 1.78                   | ~1,778            | 88,900          | 177,800          |

If we calibrate by assuming tau_int(L=12) ~ 1000 sweeps (a typical value for lattice gauge simulations at moderate coupling):

**For L=16 with 40k thermalization:**
- Number of autocorrelation times: 40,000 / 1,778 ~ 22 tau
- **This is marginally sufficient.** Standard practice requires 50-100 tau for reliable thermalization.
- At 22 tau, the system may not have fully decorrelated from the initial (typically ordered or random) configuration.

#### Recommended Thermalization for L=16

| Standard        | Required sweeps | Factor increase |
|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 50 tau (minimum)| ~89,000         | 2.2x current    |
| 100 tau (safe)  | ~178,000        | 4.5x current    |
| 200 tau (conservative) | ~356,000 | 8.9x current   |

**Recommendation: Run L=16 with at least 100,000 thermalization sweeps (and preferably 160,000-200,000) to obtain a reliable result.**

#### Using z = 2.2 (more realistic for pure Metropolis):

    tau_int(L=16) / tau_int(L=12) = (16/12)^{2.2} = 1.333^{2.2} ~ 1.92

This gives tau_int(L=16) ~ 1,920 and the 40k thermalization provides only ~21 tau.

Required for 50 tau: ~96,000 sweeps
Required for 100 tau: ~192,000 sweeps

### 2.3 Hypothesis B: Nearby Phase Transition (RULED OUT)

#### Compact U(1) in 4D

The confinement-deconfinement phase transition for 4D compact U(1) with Wilson action occurs at:

    beta_c(U(1)) = 1.011128(11)

(Confirmed by recent high-statistics Monte Carlo studies including the 2024 tricriticality paper arXiv:2404.15907.)

At beta = 3.80, the simulation is operating at nearly 4x the critical coupling, deep in the Coulomb (deconfined) phase. There is no nearby phase transition or crossover that could explain the L=16 anomaly.

**Note on recent developments:** The 2024 paper by Arnold et al. found evidence for a more complex phase structure including a possible tricritical point at T/K_0 ~ 0.19 and hints of a second tricritical point at T/K_0 ~ 0.05. However, these features are all near beta_c ~ 1.01, far from beta = 3.80.

#### SU(2) in 4D

For SU(2) lattice gauge theory, the crossover/phase transition is at:

    beta_c(SU(2)) ~ 2.3

At beta_SU(2) = 22.80, the simulation is at roughly 10x the critical coupling, extremely deep in the weak-coupling (deconfined) perturbative regime. No bulk phase transition is remotely nearby.

**Verdict: Phase transition effects can be completely ruled out as an explanation for the L=16 anomaly.**

### 2.4 Hypothesis C: Finite-Size Crossover

At L=16, the physical box size is larger, and the simulation may probe length scales where:

1. The effective coupling runs differently
2. Topological modes (monopoles, instantons) become important
3. The lattice-to-continuum mapping changes character

However, for beta = 3.80 (well into weak coupling), the correlation length xi is relatively short compared to the box size for all lattice sizes considered. The ratio L/xi should improve monotonically with L, making crossover effects unlikely.

**Partial assessment:** While not impossible, a finite-size crossover at L=16 would be unusual at this coupling. The underthermalisation hypothesis is far more likely.

### 2.5 Diagnostic Tests

To distinguish between underthermalisation and a genuine finite-size effect:

1. **Hot/cold start comparison:** Run L=16 from both ordered and disordered starts. If they agree after 40k sweeps, thermalization is adequate. If they differ, more sweeps are needed.

2. **Thermalization trajectory:** Plot the observable of interest (alpha proxy) vs Monte Carlo time. Look for:
   - A clear drift during the first N sweeps (indicates incomplete thermalization)
   - Flat plateau after some equilibration time (indicates adequate thermalization)

3. **Binning analysis:** Compute the statistical error as a function of bin size. If the error keeps growing with bin size, autocorrelations are not fully captured.

4. **Run at 80k, 120k, 160k thermalization:** If the L=16 result moves toward the L=12 result (closer to physical alpha), underthermalisation is confirmed.

5. **Measure tau_int directly:** Compute the autocorrelation function of the measured observable and extract tau_int. This gives a direct answer rather than relying on scaling estimates.

### 2.6 Expected Resolution

If underthermalisation is the culprit, increasing from 40k to 160k thermalization sweeps should:

- Provide ~90 tau of thermalization (adequate)
- Allow the L=16 result to converge toward the L=12 value or better
- Restore the expected finite-size scaling pattern

The finite-size scaling expectation for well-thermalised data would be:

    alpha(L) = alpha_phys + c * L^{-omega}

where omega is a correction-to-scaling exponent (typically 1-2 for gauge theories). The L=6,8,10,12 data (excluding L=16) should be checked for consistency with such a form to predict the L=16 target value.

### 2.7 Computational Cost Estimate

If 40k sweeps takes time T_40k, then:

- 160k sweeps: 4 * T_40k
- Plus measurement sweeps scale as L^4 per sweep and L^{2z} for decorrelation
- Total cost for L=16 at 160k thermalization: approximately 4-5x the current L=16 run time

This is computationally feasible and should be the next step before drawing conclusions about the L=16 scaling behavior.
