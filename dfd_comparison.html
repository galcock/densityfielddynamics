DFD — Comparative Frameworks

Several colleagues have asked how Density Field Dynamics (DFD) relates to existing theories. This page provides a compact, opinionated comparison across optical, scalar–tensor, modified-gravity, and GR-based approaches. Conceptually, DFD is closest to Fermat’s principle (least optical path) and Einstein’s 1911–1912 variable-c program. In contrast to GR’s curvature-first ontology, DFD starts from a single scalar refractive field ψ on flat ℝ³.

DFD (Alcock, 2024–): ψ(x) sets both geometry and dynamics on flat ℝ³. Photons see n = eψ (so one-way c₁ = c e−ψ), and matter accelerates with a = (c²/2)∇ψ. One field, GR’s classic optical and 1PN tests recovered, with explicit sector-resolved LPI experiments proposed as decisive falsifiers.

Framework Comparison — Ranked by Conceptual Proximity to DFD

The “similarity” scores below are a qualitative assessment of how closely each framework’s core mechanism tracks DFD’s refractive scalar-field picture, not a verdict on empirical success or overall value. “Least-action optics” refers specifically to Fermat’s principle (δ∫n ds = 0), the variational foundation of geometric optics.

Rank Theory / Model Similarity Core Mechanism Relation to DFD (ψ-based refractive field)
1 Density Field Dynamics (Alcock, 2024–) 100 / 100 Single scalar ψ defines both n = eψ and a = (c²/2)∇ψ. Baseline framework: curvature is not fundamental; all gravity/optics emerge from ψ and its sourcing.
2 Fermat’s Principle (1662) 70 / 100 Light follows extremal optical path: δ∫n(x) ds = 0. DFD essentially promotes n(x) to n = eψ(x) with ψ dynamically sourced. Fermat provides the variational backbone of the optical sector.
3 Einstein’s Variable-c Model (1911–1912) 65 / 100 c varies with gravitational potential; early optical-gravity program. Close conceptual ancestor: DFD can be seen as completing this program with explicit ψ-field equations and matter coupling, while enforcing energy bookkeeping.
4 Brans–Dicke Scalar–Tensor Theory (1961) 45 / 100 Scalar field φ modifies spacetime curvature (parameter ω). Shares “scalar gravity” flavor, but the scalar lives on top of GR geometry. DFD instead removes curvature and lets ψ fully replace gμν as the gravitational degree of freedom.
5 MOND / TeVeS (Milgrom 1983; Bekenstein 2004) 40 / 100 Non-linear Poisson or multi-field relativistic extensions to capture low-acceleration galaxy dynamics. Phenomenology overlap: DFD’s μ(|∇ψ|/a*) ≈ x regime reproduces RAR/Tully–Fisher scaling without tuning. Ontology differs: DFD anchors the modification in ψ-controlled optics, not in ad hoc accelerations.
6 Emergent Gravity (Verlinde, 2016) 35 / 100 Entropic/holographic origin for effective gravity and dark-matter-like behavior. Shares the goal of explaining galaxy-scale anomalies without particle dark matter. Mechanism is statistical and information-theoretic, rather than optical/variational via a refractive scalar field.
7 River Model of Space (Hamilton & Lisle, 2008) 30 / 100 “Flowing space” coordinate picture inside GR for black holes. Provides an intuitive visualization of GR metrics. DFD instead treats the one-way phase velocity c₁ = c e−ψ as a physical flow in a nondispersive band, not a coordinate choice.
8 Gordon Optical Metric (1923) 25 / 100 Effective metric in a refractive medium: gopt = g + (1 − n−2) u ⊗ u. Important for analog gravity and light-in-media. However, it presumes an underlying GR metric gμν. DFD, by contrast, takes the optical metric as fundamental and ties it directly to ψ.
9 Eddington’s Optical Analogy (1919) 20 / 100 Refractive-picture explanation of light bending in GR. Valuable historical analogy but not a standalone field theory. DFD formalizes what was previously a heuristic picture into an explicit ψ-field with sourcing and tests.
10 Tired-Light Hypotheses (Zwicky, 1929, and variants) 15 / 100 Redshift as photon energy loss over distance (non-expanding universe). Shares the idea that optics can bias cosmological inference, but lacks a consistent field equation, local tests, or a connection to gravity. DFD instead uses ψ-driven n(x) to bias optical path lengths in a controlled way.
11 General Relativity (Einstein, 1915) 10 / 100 Gravitation encoded in spacetime curvature gμν; light follows null geodesics of gμν. GR is empirically superb in tested regimes but conceptually orthogonal for this specific comparison: it begins with curvature as fundamental, whereas DFD replaces curvature with ψ and recovers GR’s classic tests via refractive optics on flat ℝ³.

In short: DFD (100) takes the refractive field ψ as fundamental. Fermat and Einstein 1911–12 (≈65–70) are the closest conceptual precursors. Scalar, MOND/TeVeS, and emergent-gravity approaches (≈35–45) share portions of the phenomenology with different underlying mechanics. Gordon and Eddington (≈20–25) are optical analogs inside GR. GR itself (10) remains the reference theory for data, but it occupies the opposite end of this very specific “refractive-scalar” axis of comparison.
